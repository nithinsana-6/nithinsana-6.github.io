SHEET 06/07Field notes
WRITING ~2 MIN READ

On chasing MTTD instead of uptime percentages

Uptime percentages are easy to game and hard to act on. 99.95% doesn't tell an on-call engineer anything to do at 2 AM — it's a lagging scorecard, not a diagnostic tool. What actually moved things for us wasn't a higher availability target; it was shortening the distance between "something broke" and "we know why."

The fix wasn't more dashboards — we already had plenty nobody looked at until something was on fire. The fix was making the right signal loud and everything else quiet: routing Datadog and Prometheus alerts by service ownership instead of by metric type, so an on-call engineer got one actionable page instead of six correlated ones. Grafana panels got rebuilt around the questions people actually ask mid-incident — "what changed in the last hour," "what's upstream of this" — instead of whatever was easiest to instrument.

Mean Time to Detect dropped 35%, not because we watched harder, but because we stopped asking engineers to do correlation in their heads during an incident. The system did the correlation; the human made the judgment call.

The same logic carried into root-cause work. "Database was slow" isn't a root cause, it's a symptom description. The practice that actually cut repeat incidents 25% was boring in the best way: every incident got a written cause, a written contributing factor, and a follow-up ticket with an owner — not a lessons-learned doc nobody reopened. Automation went where the follow-ups kept landing, which beats guessing at what to build next.

None of this is exotic. It's mostly discipline: instrument for the question you'll actually ask under pressure, and close the loop after every incident instead of half-closing it. The tooling matters less than whether the team uses it in the fifteen minutes it's on fire.
